## Guiding Principles

### English Variant

Judicial justice is the outcome produced by a legal process. [^3] It moves through a fixed pipeline:

```
Procedure → Trial → Verdict → Relief
```

At each stage, it is governed by rules of evidence, jurisdiction, admissibility, and procedure. [^4] It serves the *case* — the matter before the court — and delivers a *conclusion*. It is **role-bound**: it recognises parties as applicant, respondent, and adjudicator, and treats each differently under the law.

However, judicial justice is structurally incomplete. [^5] It does not — and by its own design cannot — account for:

- The **pain and suffering** endured during the process itself
- The **mental stigma** that attaches regardless of verdict
- The **years and life irreversibly consumed** by the process
- The **social erasure** that follows even an acquittal

A judicial outcome closes a file. It does not restore what the process took.

### Absolute Justice

Absolute Justice is not a legal outcome. It is a **universal standard** — position-neutral and role-blind. [^6] It does not recognise the titles of applicant, respondent, pleader, or judge as conferring immunity or differential standing before the standard of truth.

Absolute Justice is **equal in its reach** — it extends accountability to every person whose action touched the matter:

- The person **falsely implicated** — who suffers before any verdict is delivered
- The **witness who misled** — whose testimony shaped an unjust outcome
- The **pleader who argued in bad faith** — whose duty was to truth before strategy
- The person who **pronounced the order** — who bears the consequence of a duty exercised or misused
- The **system itself** — which cannot claim to be the exclusive remedy for the harm it caused

This is the doctronial gap that no modern judicial system formally closes: **a system cannot be both the cause of damage and the sole and complete remedy for it.** [^7] When a court corrects its own error after thirty or forty years, it declares justice served. But the declaration is procedural — not absolute.

## Objective

The judicial relief is incomplete and insufficient for a true justice.

The terms justice do not fit in the context of jurisprudence rather it is rooted deeply in the Karma and Dharma. The symbol of Ma'at [^8] reflects no ambiguity and is used as an indirect reference in the modern judicial system — while the Egyptian Ma'at symbol reflects the idea of afterlife justice: weighing the heart of a person with a feather [^9] to decide the person's further consequences — to serve a complete justice based on evidence that is equally applicable to everyone and relied merely on the truth (heart) and the evidence (feather) rather than jurisprudence.

The critical point is that Ma'at's feather does not ask *what role did you hold* — it weighs the truth of every action regardless of the title of the person who performed it. [^10] A judge, a false witness, a corrupt pleader — all stand equally before the feather. This is Absolute Justice. This is what the modern judicial system, by its own structural design, is incapable of delivering.

The traditional judicial system often considered as "modern" yet follows the same traditional procedure. [^11] In the modern context, even when the settled principals:

> *"The procedure is the handmaid, and not the mistress, of justice"* [^12]

> *"Processual law is not to be a tyrant but a servant, not an obstruction but an aid to justice. Procedural prescriptions are the handmaid and not the mistress, a lubricant, not a resistant in the administration of justice"* [^13]

...these maxims acknowledge that procedure must serve justice — yet the practice reveals that procedure *becomes* the ceiling, not merely the path. The process itself inflicts cost — in years, in dignity, in mental health, in social standing — and that cost is never formally entered into the ledger of justice. [^14]

The core principle is *"what is a complete justice"* or *"what is an ambiguity-less justice"* — especially when mere change in face, place and time, the laws well codified are redefined to fit the situation rather than using the law to see if the situation fits as per the law. And not so that the legislative body needs assistance in refiling the laws. [^15]

Interestingly, often most countries rely on the Apex Court for interpretation [^16] which in itself is incomplete. A law shall be clear, well defined so that everyone should understand the law. [^17] Especially in a country where debate on a bill before it becomes an act goes with regressive discussion and once codified, seeking interpretation contradicts the refinement of the judicial and legislative system. [^18]

## Illustrative Hypothesis — The Wrongful Imprisonment

> *A person is charged under a serious penal provision and imprisoned. After thirty to forty years, the judicial system finds the conviction was not valid. To uphold justice, the person is released. The release may sound as justice — but the sacrifice was never counted. Financial assistance cannot restore what was taken.*
>
> — Ratio Juris

This hypothesis is not merely illustrative — it is a structural proof. [^19]

Under **Judicial Justice:** the release is the remedy. The case is closed. Justice is declared served.

Under **Absolute Justice:** the following remain unaddressed and unaccounted:

| What Was Taken | What Judicial Justice Restores |
|---|---|
| 30–40 years of life | Nothing |
| Mental health and psychological damage | Nothing |
| Social identity and relationships | Nothing |
| Professional and financial life | Partial monetary compensation at best |
| The stigma that persists after acquittal | Nothing |
| Accountability of those who caused the wrongful conviction | Rarely, if ever |

The person who falsely implicated, the investigator who fabricated, the court that convicted on insufficient evidence — these actors are not placed before the same standard they imposed. That asymmetry is the precise point at which judicial justice and absolute justice diverge. [^20]

A system that exempts itself from its own standard has not delivered justice — it has delivered a **conclusion.**

## Karma, Dharma and Absolute Justice — The Universal Reading

**Karma** and **Dharma** are used here as universal principles — not as the property of any religion, culture, or civilisation. [^21] Every human society has independently arrived at these two concepts under different names:

- **Cause and consequence** — the idea that every action produces an accountable result, regardless of the actor's title or position *(Karma)*
- **Duty rightly discharged** — the idea that every person in a position of authority carries an obligation whose breach is itself an act with consequence *(Dharma)*

These are not theological claims. They are **moral physics** — observed universally, named differently across cultures, and embodied most anciently in the Egyptian Ma'at. [^22]

*Sarthaq Justice* — the author's coined term — is justice that satisfies both: it accounts for consequence fully and holds duty universally. It is the standard against which both the wrongfully convicted and the one who convicted him are measured equally.

**This is Absolute Justice. Judicial Justice is its incomplete approximation.**

## Footnotes & References

[^1]: **Righteous Action and Duty as Universal Foundations.** The concept that justice must be grounded in correct action and duty is not exclusive to any one tradition. Aristotle's *dikaiosyne* in *Nicomachean Ethics*, Book V (350 BCE) frames justice as a virtuous disposition toward correct conduct — a parallel to the universal Karma-Dharma framework used here. *See:* Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics*, Book V, trans. W.D. Ross (1908), Oxford University Press.

[^2]: **Sarthaq (सार्थक) — Original Coinage.** The term *Sarthaq Justice* is coined by the author — Ratio Juris. *Sarthaq* in Sanskrit and Hindi denotes *purposeful, meaningful, worthwhile* — deliberately contrasted with justice that is merely procedurally valid but substantively hollow.

[^3]: **Judicial Justice as Process-Bound.** Jurisprudence has long distinguished between *formal justice* (correct application of procedure) and *substantive justice* (morally correct outcomes). *See:* John Rawls, *A Theory of Justice* (1971), Harvard University Press, §§ 10–11 — distinguishing *perfect*, *imperfect*, and *pure procedural justice.*

[^4]: **Procedural Governance of Judicial Outcomes.** The binding nature of evidentiary and procedural rules in shaping (and limiting) judicial outcomes is foundational in common law. *See:* Sir William Blackstone, *Commentaries on the Laws of England* (1765–1769), Vol. 3, Ch. 20.

[^5]: **Structural Incompleteness of Judicial Relief.** The distinction between relief granted and justice achieved is acknowledged in jurisprudential discourse. *See:* Roscoe Pound, *"The Limits of Effective Legal Action"* (1917), 27 International Journal of Ethics 150 — wherein Pound argues that law's capacity to remedy social and moral injury is inherently constrained.

[^6]: **Position-Neutral Justice.** The philosophical concept that justice must be blind to status — including the status of the administrator of justice — is implicit in natural law but rarely codified. *See:* John Finnis, *Natural Law and Natural Rights* (1980), Oxford University Press, Ch. I — on universal moral standards that apply regardless of institutional position.

[^7]: **System as Cause and Sole Remedy — The Structural Contradiction.** The impossibility of a system being its own complete corrective is a recognised problem in constitutional theory. *See:* Lon L. Fuller, *The Morality of Law* (1964), Yale University Press, Ch. 2 — on the internal contradictions of legal systems that cannot self-correct adequately.

[^8]: **Ma'at — Universal Symbol of Objective Justice.** Ma'at was the ancient Egyptian principle and deity of truth, cosmic order, and justice — predating all modern legal systems. Her scales are the direct ancestor of the blindfolded Themis/Justitia used in Western courthouses today. *See:* Jan Assmann, *Ma'at: Gerechtigkeit und Unsterblichkeit im Alten Ägypten* (1990); E.A. Wallis Budge, *The Gods of the Egyptians* (1904), Vol. 1.

[^9]: **Weighing of the Heart (*Psychostasia*).** Described in the *Egyptian Book of the Dead* (*Papyrus of Ani*, c. 1250 BCE), Chapter 125 — the heart of the deceased is weighed against the feather of Ma'at before Osiris in the Hall of Two Truths. No role, title, or social position exempted any person from this weighing. *See:* E.A. Wallis Budge (trans.), *The Book of the Dead* (1895), British Museum Press.

[^10]: **Role-Blindness of Absolute Justice.** The principle that those who administer justice must themselves be subject to it is foundational in natural law and echoed in constitutional accountability doctrines. *See:* Thomas Paine, *The Rights of Man* (1791), Part I — "All men are equal before the law."

[^11]: **Modernity of Form, Antiquity of Substance.** Legal historians note that contemporary legal procedure is structurally rooted in Roman and medieval common law traditions. *See:* Frederick Pollock & F.W. Maitland, *The History of English Law Before the Time of Edward I* (1895), Cambridge University Press, Vol. 1.

[^12]: **"Procedure is handmaid, not mistress."** Settled legal maxim in Indian jurisprudence affirming that procedural law is subordinate to substantive justice. *See:* **Vinod Kumar v. State of Punjab**, (2015) 3 SCC 220 (Supreme Court of India); **Shreenath v. Rajesh**, AIR 1998 SC 1827.

[^13]: **"Procedure is meant to get justice, not to bar."** *See:* **Sangram Singh v. Election Tribunal**, AIR 1955 SC 425 (Supreme Court of India) — locus classicus on the purpose of procedural law; **State of Punjab v. Shamlal Murari**, (1976) 1 SCC 719.

[^14]: **The Cost of Process as Unaccounted Loss.** The psychological, social, and temporal cost inflicted by the legal process itself — regardless of outcome — is largely unrecognised in formal relief structures. *See:* Marc Galanter, *"Why the 'Haves' Come Out Ahead"* (1974), 9 Law & Society Review 95 — on systemic disadvantages built into legal process.

[^15]: **Law Interpreted to Fit Circumstances — Judicial Legislation Critique.** *See:* Montesquieu, *The Spirit of the Laws* (1748), Book XI, Ch. 6 — on separation of powers; **I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu**, (2007) 2 SCC 1 (Supreme Court of India) — on limits of judicial interpretation.

[^16]: **Apex Court Dependence on Interpretation.** Over-reliance on apex judicial interpretation as a substitute for legislative clarity reflects a structural inadequacy. *See:* **Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala**, (1973) 4 SCC 225; Alexander Hamilton, *Federalist No. 78* (1788) — on judicial interpretation as a limited, not supreme, power.

[^17]: **Clarity of Law (*Lex Clara*) and Rule of Law.** *See:* A.V. Dicey, *Introduction to the Study of the Law of the Constitution* (1885), Ch. 4; UN General Assembly Resolution 67/1 (2012), para. 2 — affirming that laws must be publicly promulgated, certain, and equally enforced.

[^18]: **Post-Codification Interpretation as Legislative Failure.** *See:* **K.P. Varghese v. Income Tax Officer**, (1981) 4 SCC 173 (Supreme Court of India); Lord Denning, *The Discipline of Law* (1979), Butterworths, Ch. 1 — on the limits and hazards of statutory construction displacing clear legislative intent.

[^19]: **Wrongful Imprisonment and the Limits of Judicial Remedy.** The inadequacy of post-acquittal remedies for wrongful conviction is a globally documented failure. *See:* Brandon L. Garrett, *Convicting the Innocent* (2011), Harvard University Press — documenting systemic irreversibility of harm in wrongful conviction cases; also **Rudul Sah v. State of Bihar**, AIR 1983 SC 1086 (Supreme Court of India) — wherein the court awarded compensation for wrongful detention, acknowledging that release alone is insufficient.

[^20]: **Asymmetric Accountability — The Core Flaw.** The absence of equivalent accountability for those who cause wrongful judicial outcomes is a structural feature, not an accident. *See:* The doctrine of judicial immunity — **Pierson v. Ray**, 386 U.S. 547 (1967) (US Supreme Court); **13 Action v. State**, establishing judicial immunity as a systemic shield that Absolute Justice, by the author's definition, does not recognise.

[^21]: **Karma and Dharma as Universal Concepts.** These principles are not the exclusive property of any religion. The concept of *cause and consequence* and *duty rightly discharged* appears universally — as *Ma'at* in Egypt, *Logos* in Greek Stoicism, *Tao* in Chinese philosophy, and *Natural Law* in Western jurisprudence. *See:* Sir Henry Maine, *Ancient Law* (1861), Ch. 1 — recognising duty-based legal systems across civilisations independent of religious origin.

[^22]: **Ma'at as the Oldest Articulation of Absolute Justice.** As the earliest recorded system of justice that was explicitly position-neutral and evidence-based — applying equally to pharaoh and peasant in the afterlife weighing — Ma'at remains the most complete historical model of what the author defines as Absolute Justice. *See:* E.A. Wallis Budge, *The Book of the Dead* (1895); Miriam Lichtheim, *Ancient Egyptian Literature*, Vol. II (1976), University of California Press.
