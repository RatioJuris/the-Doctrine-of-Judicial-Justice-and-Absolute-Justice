## Explanation

The philosophy articulates that true and meaningful justice is rooted in righteous action and duty, transcending external influences and upholding the inherent, objective principles of truth. [^1]

The word *Sarthaq* (सार्थक) means *purposeful* and *meaningful* — and is deliberately chosen to distinguish what justice ought to be from what judicial systems procedurally deliver. [^2]

---

## The Central Distinction — Judicial Justice and Absolute Justice

### Judicial Justice

Judicial justice is the outcome produced by a legal process. [^3] It moves through a fixed pipeline:

```
Procedure → Trial → Verdict → Relief
```

At each stage, it is governed by rules of evidence, jurisdiction, admissibility, and procedure. [^4] It serves the *case* — the matter before the court — and delivers a *conclusion*. It is **role-bound**: it recognises parties as applicant, respondent, and adjudicator, and treats each differently under the law.

However, judicial justice is structurally incomplete. [^5] It does not — and by its own design cannot — account for:

- The **pain and suffering** endured during the process itself
- The **mental stigma** that attaches regardless of verdict
- The **years and life irreversibly consumed** by the process
- The **social erasure** that follows even an acquittal

A judicial outcome closes a file. It does not restore what the process took.

### Absolute Justice

Absolute Justice is not a legal outcome. It is a **universal standard** — position-neutral and role-blind. [^6] It does not recognise the titles of applicant, respondent, pleader, or judge as conferring immunity or differential standing before the standard of truth.

Absolute Justice is **equal in its reach** — it extends accountability to every person whose action touched the matter:

- The person **falsely implicated** — who suffers before any verdict is delivered
- The **witness who misled** — whose testimony shaped an unjust outcome
- The **pleader who argued in bad faith** — whose duty was to truth before strategy
- The person who **pronounced the order** — who bears the consequence of a duty exercised or misused
- The **system itself** — which cannot claim to be the exclusive remedy for the harm it caused

This is the philosophical gap that no modern judicial system formally closes: **a system cannot be both the cause of damage and the sole and complete remedy for it.** [^7] When a court corrects its own error after thirty or forty years, it declares justice served. But the declaration is procedural — not absolute.

## Objective

The judicial relief is incomplete and insufficient for a true justice.

The terms justice do not fit in the context of jurisprudence rather it is rooted deeply in the Karma and Dharma. The symbol of Ma'at [^8] reflects no ambiguity and is used as an indirect reference in the modern judicial system — while the Egyptian Ma'at symbol reflects the idea of afterlife justice: weighing the heart of a person with a feather [^9] to decide the person's further consequences — to serve a complete justice based on evidence that is equally applicable to everyone and relied merely on the truth (heart) and the evidence (feather) rather than jurisprudence.

The critical point is that Ma'at's feather does not ask *what role did you hold* — it weighs the truth of every action regardless of the title of the person who performed it. [^10] A judge, a false witness, a corrupt pleader — all stand equally before the feather. This is Absolute Justice. This is what the modern judicial system, by its own structural design, is incapable of delivering.

The traditional judicial system often considered as "modern" yet follows the same traditional procedure. [^11] In the modern context, even when the settled principals:

> *"The procedure is handmaid, and not mistress"* [^12]

> *"The procedure is meant to get the justice and not to bar"* [^13]

...these maxims acknowledge that procedure must serve justice — yet the practice reveals that procedure *becomes* the ceiling, not merely the path. The process itself inflicts cost — in years, in dignity, in mental health, in social standing — and that cost is never formally entered into the ledger of justice. [^14]

The core principle is *"what is a complete justice"* or *"what is an ambiguity-less justice"* — especially when mere change in face, place and time, the laws well codified are redefined to fit the situation rather than using the law to see if the situation fits as per the law. And not so that the legislative body needs assistance in refiling the laws. [^15]

Interestingly, often most countries rely on the Apex Court for interpretation [^16] which in itself is incomplete. A law shall be clear, well defined so that everyone should understand the law. [^17] Especially in a country where debate on a bill before it becomes an act goes with regressive discussion and once codified, seeking interpretation contradicts the refinement of the judicial and legislative system. [^18]

## Illustrative Hypothesis — The Wrongful Imprisonment

> *A person is charged under a serious penal provision and imprisoned. After thirty to forty years, the judicial system finds the conviction was not valid. To uphold justice, the person is released. The release may sound as justice — but the sacrifice was never counted. Financial assistance cannot restore what was taken.*
>
> — Ronav Isrgrajan Narayanadas

This hypothesis is not merely illustrative — it is a structural proof. [^19]

Under **Judicial Justice:** the release is the remedy. The case is closed. Justice is declared served.

Under **Absolute Justice:** the following remain unaddressed and unaccounted:

| What Was Taken | What Judicial Justice Restores |
|---|---|
| 30–40 years of life | Nothing |
| Mental health and psychological damage | Nothing |
| Social identity and relationships | Nothing |
| Professional and financial life | Partial monetary compensation at best |
| The stigma that persists after acquittal | Nothing |
| Accountability of those who caused the wrongful conviction | Rarely, if ever |

The person who falsely implicated, the investigator who fabricated, the court that convicted on insufficient evidence — these actors are not placed before the same standard they imposed. That asymmetry is the precise point at which judicial justice and absolute justice diverge. [^20]

A system that exempts itself from its own standard has not delivered justice — it has delivered a **conclusion.**

## Karma, Dharma and Absolute Justice — The Universal Reading

**Karma** and **Dharma** are used here as universal principles — not as the property of any religion, culture, or civilisation. [^21] Every human society has independently arrived at these two concepts under different names:

- **Cause and consequence** — the idea that every action produces an accountable result, regardless of the actor's title or position *(Karma)*
- **Duty rightly discharged** — the idea that every person in a position of authority carries an obligation whose breach is itself an act with consequence *(Dharma)*

These are not theological claims. They are **moral physics** — observed universally, named differently across cultures, and embodied most anciently in the Egyptian Ma'at. [^22]

*Sarthaq Justice* — the author's coined term — is justice that satisfies both: it accounts for consequence fully and holds duty universally. It is the standard against which both the wrongfully convicted and the one who convicted him are measured equally.

**This is Absolute Justice. Judicial Justice is its incomplete approximation.**

## Extended Doctrine — What Cannot Be Given or Reinstated Cannot Be Taken

> *"What cannot be given or reinstated cannot be taken."*
>
> — Ronav Isrgrajan Narayanadas

This principle is the logical extension of the distinction between Judicial Justice and Absolute Justice. It operates as a doctrinal ceiling — a test by which any punitive measure, any relief, and any judicial outcome may be evaluated for its intrinsic legitimacy. If a judicial system cannot restore what it proposes to take, it has no jurisprudential authority to take it. The power to punish must be bounded by the capacity to account for what punishment irreversibly destroys.

### I. Death Punishment — The Mind, Not the Body, Commits a Crime

The imposition of capital punishment is, under this doctrine, unjustifiable — not merely on humanitarian grounds, but on a fundamental jurisprudential one: **it is the mind that commits a crime, not the body.**

A body without a functioning mind does not offend. It does not plan, intend, deceive, or act with purpose. The entire architecture of criminal law — *mens rea*, *actus reus*, intent, premeditation, knowledge, recklessness — is premised on the acknowledgement that the mind is the seat of culpability. [^23] Criminal liability attaches to the person's mental state first, and to the physical act second. Without the former, the latter is legally and morally inert.

If it is conceded — as criminal law itself concedes — that the mind is the operative agent of crime, then the destruction of the body is not the destruction of what offended. It is, at most, the elimination of the vessel. And the elimination of a vessel that cannot be restored — whose life, once taken, admits of no reinstatement — violates the foundational principle: **what cannot be given or reinstated cannot be taken.**

### II. The Contradiction of Competency and Capital Punishment

The judicial system's own doctrine of **competency of mind** deepens this contradiction further. [^24] Before any person can be tried, convicted, or sentenced, the system requires that they be mentally competent — capable of understanding the proceedings against them and participating in their own defence. A person found incompetent of mind is not tried. They are not sentenced. The system recognises, in that moment, that the mind is the subject of justice, not merely the body.

Yet capital punishment — the ultimate sanction — destroys the body. It does not address the mind. It does not correct it, reform it, or render it accountable in any operative sense. If the system acknowledges that a person without a competent mind cannot be subject to its proceedings, it implicitly acknowledges that the mind — not the body — is the proper subject of accountability. To then execute the body is to punish the wrong subject entirely. This is not a contradiction at the margins of the doctrine; it is a contradiction at its centre.

The judicial system, by its own internal logic, cannot simultaneously hold that:

- A person without a competent mind is exempt from trial; **and**
- The appropriate ultimate punishment for the most serious crimes is the destruction of the body

These two positions are irreconcilable. One treats the mind as the subject of justice. The other ignores it entirely.

**Punishment is vital to a functioning judicial system.** The author does not contest that. Accountability, deterrence, and the protection of society are legitimate and necessary functions of penal law. But death — as a form of punishment — is not justified under any reading of Absolute Justice, and the principle reaffirms itself: *what cannot be given or reinstated cannot be taken.*

### III. The Procedural Collapse of Civil Justice — From Interpretation to Mediation

The same principle — *what cannot be given or reinstated cannot be taken* — applies with equal force to civil disputes. Here, however, the failure of Absolute Justice is not delivered in a single irreversible act. It is delivered incrementally, across years and decades, through a process that begins as adjudication and ends as something the system was never designed to be.

Civil disputes — particularly those involving property, family, commercial relationships, and personal rights — routinely consume years of procedural life before reaching any resolution. [^25] The trajectory is consistent and observable:

```
Dispute → Filing → Prolonged Hearing → Procedural Delays → Settlement Pressure → Mediation
```

What this trajectory reveals is a **structural demotion of the judicial function**. The court, designed as an interpreter of law and adjudicator of rights, becomes first an overseer of procedure, and then — when the burden of its own backlog becomes unmanageable — a facilitator of compromise. Mediation is positioned as a solution. It is not. It is an acknowledgement of failure.

**Mediation is not justice. It is the redistribution of an unresolved dispute under a different name.** In a healthy judicial system, mediation may serve a supplementary role where both parties genuinely seek a negotiated resolution. But when mediation is structurally induced — when it is the product of systemic delay, procedural exhaustion, and the court's own inability to deliver a timely adjudicated outcome — it is not a resolution. It is a forced settlement dressed as consent. The party with greater endurance, greater financial resource, or lesser vulnerability to delay wins not on the merits of their case but on their capacity to outlast the process. [^26]

### IV. The Family Court — A Lens That Cannot See What It Causes

The family court exemplifies the gap between judicial procedure and Absolute Justice with particular precision. Consider the standard disposition in a matrimonial separation matter: a court directs one party — most frequently the husband — to pay maintenance or livelihood compensation to the other. [^27] This is presented as justice. It reduces the burden on the judicial system. It provides a monetised resolution to a relational rupture.

But the judicial lens, by its design, does not see — and is not equipped to see — the following:

A woman separated and living alone is not merely a financial category. She is a person experiencing a critical and irreversible phase of her life. Her fertility, her social positioning, her relational architecture, and her psychological continuity are all in motion during the period that the judicial process consumes. The years spent in litigation are years not spent rebuilding. The months awaiting hearing dates are months in which her circumstances compound without adjudication. The maintenance order, when it finally comes, addresses none of this. [^28]

**For the woman:** the maintenance payment is not justice. It is a financial approximation of a life that cannot be restored. The critical phase of her life — consumed by the process — is not compensable by any monetary order a court can make.

**For the man:** the maintenance order is not justice either. It is a continuing financial obligation that attaches regardless of the underlying circumstances of the separation, and it carries with it a social stigma that the order itself cannot address or remove.

Neither party has received Absolute Justice. Both have received a **procedure that concluded.**

Furthermore — and this extends the doctrine to its biological dimension — even in the natural world, animals do not instinctively claim, raise, or accept responsibility for offspring that are not biologically their own. The instinct of paternity is grounded in biological reality. Yet the judicial system, by design, is capable of directing a man to bear financial and legal responsibility for children whose biological paternity he did not contribute to — a direction issued not on the basis of biological truth but on the procedural status of a legal relationship. [^29] This is not a failure of sensitivity. It is a structural impossibility: **the system, by its own design, cannot deliver Absolute Justice — it can only complete a procedure.**

---

### V. The Award as Procedure — Relief Without Justice

This leads to the doctrine's concluding observation on the present state of judicial delivery:

**In the present judicial system, the delivery of an award — whether relief, compensation, acquittal, or order — is itself a procedure rather than justice. It is encoded as such in the legal framework.**

The award marks the end of a case. It does not mark the restoration of what was lost, the accounting of what was taken, or the application of a standard that is equal in its reach to all who participated in the matter. The award is the system's declaration that it has completed its function — not that justice has been done.

This distinction — between the **completion of a procedure** and the **delivery of justice** — is the central claim of this doctrine. Judicial Justice, as defined throughout this document, is the completion of a procedure. Absolute Justice is the standard against which that completion must ultimately be measured. The gap between them is not a defect that reform can close. It is structural. It is inherent. And it must be named — clearly, and without equivocation — for what it is.

*What cannot be given or reinstated cannot be taken. The judicial system, by its own design, regularly takes what it cannot restore — years, life, biological reality, irreversible time — and calls the resulting procedure: justice.*
